An Introduction to WCAG and a visual accessibility checklist based on WCAG guidelines

For designers, one of the most important guidelines to look at is The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), a set of international guidelines that help make websites and apps more accessible to individuals with disabilities. These guidelines provide recommendations on how to design and develop websites and apps in a way that makes them easier for people with disabilities to use. By following these guidelines, website and app creators can ensure that their content is accessible to a wider audience, including those with visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive impairments.

History

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) were first developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) in 1999 in response to the growing need for web accessibility. The first version of the guidelines, known as WCAG 1.0, was published in 1999. This version of the guidelines provided a set of principles and guidelines for making web content accessible to people with disabilities, as well as a set of checkpoints for evaluating the accessibility of web content. WCAG 2.0 was published in 2008, and WCAG 2.1 in 2018.

4 Principles

There are four principles of accessibility in WCAG. All guidelines are organized according to these principles.

  1. Perceivable

    2. Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

    One example of how people may perceive content in different ways is a person with color blindness viewing a website or app that uses color to convey information. A person with normal color vision may be able to easily understand the information being conveyed by the use of different colors, but a person with color blindness may not be able to differentiate between certain colors and may miss out on important information. Therefore, the website or app could use other visual indicators, such as patterns or shapes, to convey the same information.

  2. Operable

    3. User interface components and navigation must be operable.

    A person without a physical disability may be able to easily use a keyboard to input text and navigate a website or app, but a person with a physical disability that affects their ability to use a keyboard may not be able to do so.

  3. Understandable

    4. Information and the operation of user interface must be understandable.

    Content creators need to make sure that the text on their website or app is easy to read and understand for the widest possible audience, including when it is read aloud by text-to-speech technology. This means using clear and simple language, avoiding complex sentence structures, and providing context or explanations for any jargon or specialized terms. By making the text on their website or app accessible to people with visual impairments or other disabilities, content creators can ensure that their content is accessible to a wider audience.

  4. Robust

    5. Content must be robust enough that it can be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

    Take a website or app that provides information about public transportation routes and schedules. A sighted person may be able to easily view this information on the website or app, but a person with a visual impairment may need to use a screen reader to access the information. To make the content accessible to people with visual impairments, the website or app must be designed in a way that allows screen reader software to accurately interpret and convey the information about the transportation routes and schedules. This may involve using clear and descriptive headings and labels, providing alt text for images, and properly formatting tables and lists.

Success Criteria

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) have three levels of compliance, which reflect the priority of support for making web content accessible to people with disabilities. The three levels are:

  • Level A

    • This is the lowest level of compliance, and it focuses on ensuring that the basic accessibility needs of people with disabilities are met. If the content on a website or app is not designed in a way that meets the requirements of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), assistive technology may not be able to accurately read, understand, or fully operate the page or view.

  • Level AA

    • This is the intermediate level of compliance, and it builds on the requirements of Level A by providing additional support for people with disabilities.

  • Level AAA

    • This is the highest level of compliance, and it provides the most comprehensive support for people with disabilities. This includes meeting all of the requirements of Levels A and AA, as well as additional requirements for making web content more accessible to people with disabilities.
