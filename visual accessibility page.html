Introduction to Visually Impaired

Who are visually impaired and why is it important for accessibility?

Why Visually Impaired?

We decided to look specifically into visually impaired people because globally, at least 2.5 billion people have a near or distance vision impairment, according to a WHO report. It is estimated that there are more than 350 million colorblind people in the world. The prevalence of colorblindness varies, but it is generally agreed that around 8% of males and 0.5% of females are affected by the condition. This means that approximately 1 in 12 males and 1 in 200 females are colorblind. Approximately 12 children with color deficiency are born per minute. Though WHO pointed out that almost half of the visual impairment cases could have been prevented, population growth and aging mean the risk that more people acquire vision impairment is now bigger than ever.

Moreover, according to the Global Report on Assistive Technology by WHO and United Nations Children’s Fund, the need for visual impairment technologies was highest among all types of assistive products. Therefore, our project focuses on visual accessibility design.

Common Types of Visual Impairment

Besides blindness, common types of visual impairment can include low vision and color blindness.

Low vision

Low vision can include partial sight in one or both eyes, poor acuity (blurry vision), tunnel vision, central field loss, and clouded vision. The things will not look sharp and clear for people with blurred vision. Tunnel vision is a vision defect where objects cannot be seen unless they are near the center of the visual field.

Color blindness

People who are color blind have trouble seeing the difference between colors, how bright the color is, and different shades of color. Different types of color blindness cause problems seeing different colors.

A. Red-green color blindness

  • Deuteranomaly makes green look more red.
  • Protanomaly makes red look more green and less bright.
  • Protanopia and deuteranopia both make you unable to tell the difference between red and green at all.

B. Blue-yellow color blindness

  • Tritanomaly makes it hard to tell the difference between blue and green, and between yellow and red.
  • Tritanopia makes you unable to tell the difference between blue and green, purple and red, and yellow and pink. It also makes colors look less bright.

C. Complete color blindness

People who acquire complete color blindness can’t see colors at all.

You can use this color blindness simulator to see how colors appear to people with different types of colorblindness. First, type the color hex code without the pound sign, and then press the Enter key. You can then move their mouse over different buttons to see the corresponding colors.
