Accessible Products

A collection of physical and digital products.

Historically, accessible products were created for people with special needs but ended up benefitting everyone. From a vegetable peeler to a typewriter, people designed innovative solutions to cater to the needs of the differently abled. We have created a list of some of these products and categorised them into two different sections - Physical Objects and Digital Products.

Physical Objects

Typewriter

Would you believe that the typewriter was invented so that a blind woman could communicate with her lover? This incredible story is behind how the typewriter was invented. Pellegrino Turri developed a typing machine so that his blind lover, Countess Carolina Fantoni, could send him love letters without the need for her to dictate to a scribe. The original machine was not preserved but the letters written by the Countess still survive! You can read more about Pellegrino on Wikipedia.

Audiobooks

Audiobooks have become a very common way for readers to access their content without looking at a device or reading a book. However, audiobooks have a long history and an interesting origin story. Talking books were one of the first applications of the phonographic when it was invented in 1877 — as Thomas Edison said, they could “speak to blind people without effort on their part. The introduction of the cassette made audiobooks much more portable, and the general public began to enjoy the convenience of listening rather than reading. Today, millions of readers enjoy their favorite books through different audiobooks and streaming apps.Source: Hackernoon article.

Braille Alphabet Lego Bricks

Lego has created a set of 250 bricks with the letters, numbers and symbols of the braille script imprinted on the stangard lego building blocks. These blocks can help blind and visually impared children learn and play at the same time. Though not available workldwide for sale, these bricks will be distributed to selected educational instutions for free. You can read more about it in this article.

New Age Digital Products

TypeCase Keyboard

Our increasing reliability of making screens sleek and seamless by removing any tactile buttons from the interface makes them inaccessible to certain groups - specifically those who are blind or have poor motor coordination. Engineer Dougie Mann built the TypeCase keyboard to address this issue. The keyboard is an external attachable phone case and has 5 physical buttons which can be used in different combinations to represent all 26 letters of the alphabet. It brings tactility back into the experience of using a digital device and greatly imrpoves the accessibility of the interface. You can read more about the TypeCase in this Dezeen article.

FingerSpelling.xyz

Ever wanted to learn the American Sign Language but did not know where to begin? Hello Monday studio has built an app that teaches ASL by looking at your hand signs through the camera. Fingerspelling.xyz helps you learn the sign language by using machine learning to track your movements and correct mistakes in real time. It is a great resources for children, teachers and students to get started with learning the ASL. You can try it for yourself here.

NaviLens

NaviLens does exactly what its title suggests. It helps visually impared people find signage that is otherwise hard to access. NaviLens was developed in Barcelona, Spain to improve the accessibility of the public transport of the city. The app uses unique barcodes that the camera can automatically and accurately scan from a distance of over 20 meters and can inform the users about what that signage says in the real world. The creators of NaviLens hope that this technology will be adopted in different domains to make all spaces accessible. Check out their official website for more information!
